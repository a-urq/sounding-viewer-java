Sounding Viewer Controls Page

Made by Amelia Urquhart

Controls

Control What it does
1 If two soundings from different times are loaded up in the viewer, displays the earlier of the two uninterpolated soundings.
2 If two soundings from different times are loaded up in the viewer, displays the time-interpolated sounding.
3 If two soundings from different times are loaded up in the viewer, displays the later of the two uninterpolated soundings.
C Shows this page.
E Toggles whether parcels and convective readouts consider entrainment. On by default. Entrainment CAPE is computed according to Peters et al. 2023.
F Toggles whether the frost point trace (dashed line, bluish-green) is shown. Off by default. Computed according to formula in Huffman and Norman 1988, Appendix B.
I Toggles whether parcels and convective readouts use irreversible adiabatic ascent. On by default. Irreversible adiabatic ascent accounts for the effects of precipitation loading. According to Peters et al. 2022, irreversible adiabatic ascent produces a more accurate estimation of CAPE and is therefore recommended.
P Shows a dialog where the user can choose the convective parcel path displayed on the Skew-T diagram.
S Shows a dialog where the users can choose the storm motion vector used for kinematics calculations.

Parcel Path Options

Surface Based

When to use: Surface based storms. May be useful in assessing tornado potential in surface based supercells.

How it's calculated: Sets the pressure, temperature, and dewpoint of the starting point to that of the environmental air at the surface.

Mixed Layer

When to use: Surface based storms. Often used as a general-purpose indicator of storm strength.

How it's calculated: Computes the average potential temperature and mixing ratio in the lowest 100 mb of the atmosphere. Sets the pressure of the starting point to that of the surface air. Computes the parcel temperature using the average potential temperature and surface pressure and the parcel dewpoint using the average mixing ratio and surface pressure.

Most Unstable

When to use: Elevated storms.

How it's calculated: Finds the pressure level with the highest Theta-E (equivalent potential temperature). Sets the pressure, temperature, and dewpoint to the environmental air at that level.

Inflow Layer

When to use: Any storm. Will display nothing if no inflow layer is found.

How it's calculated: Computes the average potential temperature and mixing ratio in the effective inflow layer. Sets the pressure of the starting point to that of the inflow base (often the surface for surface-based storms). Computes the parcel temperature using the average potential temperature and surface pressure and the parcel dewpoint using the average mixing ratio and inflow base pressure.

Storm Motion Vector Options

Left Moving

When to use: Supercells with clockwise-rotating mesocyclones.

How it's calculated: Uses the Left Moving storm motion vector as calculated by Bunkers et. al. (2000).

Mean Wind

When to use: Non-supercellular storms.

How it's calculated: The non-weighted average of the wind from the surface to a 6 km altitude.

Right Moving

When to use: Supercells with counterclockwise-rotating mesocyclones. Most supercells in the Northern Hemisphere are like this.

How it's calculated: Uses the Right Moving storm motion vector as calculated by Bunkers et. al. (2000).

Corfidi Upshear

When to use: Backbuilding Mesoscale Convective Systems.

How it's calculated: Uses the Upwind-Propagating vector as calculated by Corfidi 2003.

Corfidi Downshear

When to use: Cold pool propagated Mesoscale Convective Systems (bow echoes).

How it's calculated: Uses the Downwind-Propagating vector as calculated by Corfidi 2003.
